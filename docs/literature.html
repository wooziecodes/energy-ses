Historical Context

1970s - Present

The last sixty years have seen waves of interest in policies relating to energy consumption, first in the 1970s as a response to the energy crisis at the time (Brounen, Kok and Quigley, 2012), before a re-emergence of interest in the early 2000s in the wake of global agreements, such as the Kyoto Protocol of 1997 (UNFCCC, 2024) and the Paris Agreement of 2015 (UNFCCC, 2020), and domestic legislation, such as the 2008 Climate Change Act and the 2013 Green Deal.

Early Research & Data Challenges

Houthakker's Pioneering Study (1951)

Houthakker carried out some of the earliest statistical analysis of factors affecting domestic energy consumption in Great Britain, aiming to produce 'a comprehensive study of the various components of electricity consumption' (Houthakker, 1951, p.359).

The problem of finding necessary data to produce comprehensive studies continues to plague energy consumption research, with existing data collection and statistical models frequently operating at either national or individual building levels (Gassar, Yun and Kim, 2019).

Current Research Landscape

Studies such as Steemers and Yun (2009) have used predictive regression models to establish strong relationships between household income and residential energy consumption. However, a 'lack of detailed micro-economic data' has led to the exclusion of social and demographic characteristics in much of the engineering literature on energy efficiency (Brounen, Kok and Quigley, 2012).

Environmental Education Impact

Osuntuyi and Lean found that while 'education worsens environmental deterioration', education level can have a 'moderating influence' on environmental detriment caused by higher income groups (Osuntuyi and Lean, 2023).

Economic Inequality & Environmental Impact

Berthe and Elie conducted one of the most comprehensive studies examining the effects of economic inequalities on environmental degradation (Uzar, 2020), identifying two key channels:

Two-Channel Framework

  • Economic Channel: Considers the impact of economic behavior on the environment
  • Political Channel: Focuses on environmental public policy

Both channels must be considered for effective public policy recommendations (Berthe and Elie, 2015).
