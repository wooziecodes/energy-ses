Overview

How does average income affect average domestic gas consumption in London boroughs, when average household size and dwelling energy efficiency are controlled for?

This study aimed to determine the key factors influencing mean domestic gas consumption (in kWh per meter) across London boroughs through an ordinary least squares normal linear regression model. The model examined the relationship between median income and gas consumption while controlling for household size and energy efficiency ratings, including interaction effects between these variables.

Key Findings

  • The model explained 67.8% of the variance in mean domestic gas consumption in different boroughs
  • For every Â£1 increase in median income (relative to borough average), gas consumption increases by 6.73 kWh
  • Higher energy efficiency ratings significantly reduce consumption (-333.53 kWh per rating point)
  • Larger household sizes substantially increase consumption (6,813 kWh per additional person)
  • The effect of income on consumption is moderated by both household size and energy efficiency

Variable Centering

To address multicollinearity and improve interpretability, median income and mean people per household were centered on their respective means. This means:

  • Coefficients represent effects relative to borough-wide averages
  • The coefficient for centered median income represents the effect when household size is at its mean
  • The coefficient for centered household size represents the effect when income is at its mean
  • The intercept represents expected gas consumption when both income and household size are at their means

This centering approach successfully reduced VIF values from >100 to <5, addressing multicollinearity concerns while maintaining interpretability.

Detailed Coefficient Interpretations

Intercept

Value: 35,150 (p<0.001)

Represents the expected gas consumption for a borough with average income and household size.

While this baseline value appears high compared to the UK average household consumption of 11,500 kWh, this discrepancy is likely due to the intercept representing a theoretical case with an energy rating of 0 (outside the actual 1-100 scale). As an extrapolation beyond our data range, this high value doesn't necessarily indicate model problems.

Centered Median Income

Coefficient: 6.7249 (p=0.001)

For a Â£1 increase in median income relative to the borough average, gas consumption increases by 6.73 kWh when other factors are held constant. This positive relationship directly addresses our research question, confirming our hypothesis that higher-income areas show increased energy consumption, even when controlling for household size and efficiency.

Centered Mean People per Household

Coefficient: 6,812.86 (p<0.001)

For each additional person in the average household size (relative to borough average), consumption increases by 6,813 kWh. This substantial effect highlights the importance of household size in determining energy consumption patterns.

Energy Efficiency Rating

Coefficient: -333.53 (p<0.001)

Each one-point improvement in energy efficiency rating reduces consumption by 333.5 kWh, demonstrating the significant impact of building efficiency on energy use.

The relationship between income and consumption strengthens with larger household sizes. The combined effect of high income and large households leads to even greater consumption than would be expected from their individual effects alone. This suggests that energy conservation strategies might need to be particularly targeted at larger, higher-income households.

Higher income amplifies the effect of energy efficiency improvements. This suggests that energy efficiency improvements might be particularly effective in higher-income areas.
