Research Context & Significance

In an era where energy costs have risen by over 60% since 2021, understanding the relationship between socioeconomic factors and energy consumption has never been more critical. With £3 billion in household energy debt affecting 2.3 million UK households, and the fuel poverty gap increasing by 67% between 2020-2023 (Committee on Fuel Poverty, 2024), our research aims to use data science to understand and address these pressing social challenges.

"While energy prices have stabilised and the energy price cap has fallen further, energy bills remain significantly higher than we have been used to. Many households have been struggling to pay for the energy they need, facing difficult decisions."

- Ofgem Affordability and Debt Report, March 2024

Why This Matters:

  • 24.7% of households in regions like Stoke-on-Trent face fuel poverty1
  • Average energy arrears of £1,761 for households without repayment plans2
  • Disproportionate impact on lower-income areas, with 35.1% of fuel poor households in private rented sector1

This quantitative analysis explores these patterns across London boroughs, using comprehensive datasets to understand how income levels, energy efficiency ratings, and consumption patterns interact. By applying advanced statistical methods, we aim to provide evidence-based insights for policy interventions and social solutions.

Research Question & Variables

Our analysis focuses on the relationship between household income and gas consumption, while controlling for key variables:

Primary Variables:

  • Dependent: Annual gas consumption (kWh)
  • Independent: Average household income
  • Control: Energy efficiency rating (EPC)
  • Control: Average household size

Using multiple regression analysis, we examine whether higher-income households consume more gas when controlling for dwelling efficiency and household size.

Data Science Approach

Data Collection

Integration of borough-level datasets from government sources, energy providers, and demographic surveys.

Statistical Analysis

Multiple regression modeling with testing of assumptions and validation procedures.

Visualization

Data visualizations to explore patterns and relationships.

Study Parameters & Expected Impact

The analysis covers the period 2021-2024, during which energy markets experienced unprecedented volatility and social pressures intensified. Our dataset incorporates:

  • Monthly gas consumption patterns across income groups
  • Detailed household income and expenditure surveys
  • Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) ratings database
  • Granular borough demographic and socioeconomic profiles

Through statistical analysis including tests for multicollinearity, heteroscedasticity, and normal distribution of residuals, we aim to:

Policy Impact

Provide data-driven insights for targeted energy support policies

Social Understanding

Quantify the relationship between income and energy consumption

Future Planning

Inform strategies for reducing energy inequality
