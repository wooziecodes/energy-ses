Research Context

Our focus on London boroughs comes at a critical time when the city faces unprecedented challenges in energy consumption and affordability. The stark contrast between affluent and struggling neighborhoods presents a unique opportunity to understand how socioeconomic factors influence energy usage patterns.

Critical Impact: Rising energy costs disproportionately affect lower-income households, with some families spending over 10% of their income on energy bills.

Research Focus

The relationship between income levels and energy consumption has long been a subject of interest among policymakers and researchers, particularly in urban settings where economic disparity and environmental concerns intersect. This research focuses on gas consumption in London, a global metropolis characterized by diverse socioeconomic conditions across its boroughs. Amid rising gas prices and growing concerns over energy efficiency, understanding the dynamics of household gas usage in relation to income is critical for developing targeted policies that balance economic equity and environmental sustainability.

Research Question & Model

The primary aim of this research is to construct and evaluate a model that explains gas consumption patterns in London households. Specifically, the model incorporates key variables such as average income, average energy efficiency of dwellings, and the average number of people per household in each borough.

Do households in boroughs with higher earners consume more gas, when average people per household and energy efficiency of dwellings are controlled for?

This research hypothesizes that higher-income households do consume more gas under these controlled conditions. By applying statistical modeling, this study delves into the interplay of income, household size, and energy efficiency, further exploring how these factors interact to influence domestic gas consumption.

Methodology Overview

To ensure the regression model produces accurate results and satisfies the assumptions of normal linear regression, comprehensive data cleaning was undertaken. This included addressing missing or extraneous data, standardizing variable formats, and ensuring consistency across datasets.

Study Limitations

Household Count

The model did not control for the number of households per borough, potentially causing disproportionate influence from boroughs with fewer households.

Appliance Data

The study was unable to account for the impact of household appliances on gas consumption due to a lack of data.

Building Types

The model did not account for residential building types, which can significantly influence energy consumption due to variations in factors such as square footage and building age.
